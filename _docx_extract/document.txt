Many of the earlier attempts at phishing could be identified by grammatical errors, generic messages, or even suspicious-looking formats. Today, through AI, the bad actors can generate highly personalized and well-structured messages at scale, evading manual detection and automated filtering. These AI-generated messages can mimic internal communication tone, organizational tone, and contextual relevance to users, building user trust and reducing the traditional warning signs. Besides the sophistication of the content itself, numerous evasion techniques-such as browser fingerprinting, interaction-dependent content delivery, and client-side cloaking-enable phishing pages to only present their malicious content to genuine users while evading automated scanners. This extends the time the attack can remain active and increases the chances of victims clicking on malicious links or providing sensitive information. Live phishing tests cannot be carried out in natural environments due to various legal, ethical, and privacy restrictions. The research project now tries to create a controlled simulation environment which models how phishing attacks spread and how different users react to them in an environment devoid of real-world consequences. CHAPTER 2 PROBLEM DEFINITION 2.1 Background and Motivation Phishing remains a very successful attack vector because of its dependence on human error. As generative AI became more advanced, phishing attacks became more sophisticated and adaptive, rendering traditional rule-based systems for phishing detection increasingly irrelevant. Attackers generate context-specific content in near real-time that allows for very targeted, scalable attacks. Lateral phishing attacks-where an attacker uses an already compromised account inside of an organization-massively amplify trust and lower suspicion for recipients. 2.2 Core Problem Empirical studies of AI-driven phishing are risky, illegal, and infeasible. Deploying phishing sites, collecting user data, and observing human behavior under live-attack conditions violates ethical and privacy regulations. Most literature only abstracts the human response to a binary outcome, such as click/no-click, without granularity in behavioral proxies (e.g., hesitation, partial form fill) or times spent evaluating content. Furthermore, the interplay between technical evasion, like cloaking, and human factors remains an area that is less explored. 2.3 Problem We Aim to Solve The project proposes a Hybrid Agent-Based Simulation framework that can mimic both human decision-making and technical evasion stages of phishing attacks. The project creates a controlled simulation of the organizational communication network for the systematic evaluation of attack dynamics, susceptibility patterns, and the effectiveness of evasion strategies without real-world risk. This addresses the absence of research environments that can safely analyze AI-enhanced phishing campaigns. CHAPTER 3 LITERATURE REVIEW 3.1 AI-Enhanced Social Engineering Recent studies have shown that LLMs have eliminated traditional phishing cues by generating high-quality and highly contextual content. Large-scale organizational studies with thousands of employees have also demonstrated that LLM-generated lateral phishing emails can achieve equal or superior open and click-through rates to those authored by human experts [1]. Also, hybrid models that infuse LLM generation with cognitive frameworks demonstrate near-human efficacy while drastically reducing the effort, cost, and expertise requirements of the attacker [3]. These findings confirm previously raised concerns over the scalability and precision of AI-powered social engineering. Such techniques include user-interaction checks, browser fingerprinting, and filtering on bot behaviour-all of which make the automated scanners far less effective. The "Golden Hour" concept illustrates how phishing campaigns take advantage of a critical time window where detection systems do not react yet. Models such as KnowPhish extend defences through multimodal knowledge graphs and LLM-derived textual intent, addressing gaps in logo-based or static detectors [5]. These insights provide the foundation to model technical evasion in the proposed simulation. Various research shows that factors like fatigue, perceived vulnerability, job pressure, and level of attention significantly impact decision-making [9]. The cognitive models provide further explanations about how users assess cues while dynamically adjusting their suspicion threshold based on context and memory [10]. However, existing simulations mainly depend on static parameters, lack specific behavioural details, and hardly include technical evasion techniques. This paper will thus unify the elements mentioned above to develop an integrated simulation model. Figure 2: Simulation framework overview CHAPTER 4 RESEARCH/TECHNOLOGIES GAPS AND CHALLENGES One such research gap is in the restricted understanding of how technical evasion strategies interact with human decision-making. The current state of the art either analyses phishing websites or user psychology separately; few works investigate their interaction. This interaction is important because, while evasion techniques are responsible for prolonging exposure, psychological factors dictate how users react within a varying context of trust, fatigue, or urgency. It is otherwise hardly possible to estimate realistic attack success rates or test defence mechanisms without an integrated framework. Defensive challenges also stem from the limitations of modern detection systems. LLM-generated content is extremely adaptable, and minor changes based on prompts can easily pass filters. Detection of unknown LLM-generated emails presents a particular challenge because such models can generate content in divergent styles. On the technical side, the real-time analysis of cloaking or behavior-based scripts requires expensive computation and may still fail against sophisticated client-side evasion. Behavioral issues further complicate matters: users are influenced by cognitive biases, contextual triggers, and organizational hierarchies-all those things that an attacker can leverage. These dynamic factors are not considered by current training programs, so their awareness strategies have to be generalized and often fail. Addressing these gaps requires a simulation model capable of integrating technical evasion with cognitive and behavioural variability. The simulated environment is a virtual organization with 235 agents with varied attributes like fatigue, perceived vulnerability, and job performance. These are based on empirical regression models from previous research. Figure 3: Clustering of participants in the no-multitasking condition 5.2 Dataset Description The cognitive component uses a curated dataset of 40 email samples, including 20 legitimate and 20 phishing emails. These samples populate the agent's long-term memory and form the basis of decision-making during simulations. Parameters such as suspicion thresholds and flawed perception levels influence how agents classify emails. The L-ĆōP ratio is deliberately skewed using duplication of legitimate email samples to reflect realistic exposure patterns where legitimate emails outnumber phishing attempts. This dataset allows the simulation to model hesitation, misclassification, and gradual susceptibility changes over time. Even though this dataset is relatively small, its purpose is to demonstrate Phase-Ćō1 feasibility and provide the foundation for a much larger, more dynamic dataset in Phase-Ćō2. The team developed a broad literature survey and identified key gaps in existing phishing detection research and developed a structured workflow for simulating attack scenarios. This has clearly brought out the need for safe, ethical, and scalable simulation environments to study AI-driven phishing attacks that cannot be experimentally deployed in real-world settings. Future work will focus on the implementation of the complete simulation architecture, further enhancement of cognitive models, integration of multimodal phishing scenarios-voice and visual attacks, and expansion of the behavioral dataset. Phase-Ćō2 will further introduce real-time analytics dashboards, threat progression based on scenarios, and reinforcement learning components to evaluate adaptive defenses. The resultant outcome will ensure more accurate and actionable insights into organizational susceptibility while helping develop targeted defensive strategies.
